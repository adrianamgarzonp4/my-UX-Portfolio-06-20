Project overview

Summary

Through qualitative and quantitative user research, I discovered that travelers have a difficult time finding information about local experiences available at their desired destinations. This reality causes them to miss authentic experiences and to waste time and money once they are at their destination.

PukkaTrip is a travel app prototype designed to alleviate the described pain point by helping travelers discover authentic local experiences.

Project Goals

  • Uncover travelers’ pain points in their trip planning process (when traveling for vacation)
  • Implement a user-centered design methodology to prototype a mobile travel app that addresses those pain points

My Role & Responsibilities

Sole UX and UI designer

Timeline 

4 weeks

Tools

Pen & paper, Figma, Photoshop, Illustrator, Adobe XD, Miro & Procreate

Methodology

I approached this problem from a place of curiosity. That curiosity turned into empathy as I interviewed users. Once I understood their main pain points, I narrowed the scope of the problem by defining a user persona and scenario. As a next step, I ideated potential solutions, prioritized, designed, and tested the user flow and visual design a couple of times. Based on the user feedback, I revised my wireframes and prototyped a minimum valuable product that addressed the users' pain points and stood out from the competition.

Creating an understanding of the scope of the problem and getting to know the users

To learn more about how travelers plan their trips, I performed 5 interviews and 23 surveys.

Questions asked

  • When traveling for vacation, what are some of the reasons why you do it?
  • How do you go about planning a trip?
  • What is the most time-consuming part about planning a vacation trip?
  • What are the most time-consuming activities at your destination?

Making sense of the interview and survey data

User insight

Finding a user insight was my first step to begin defining the problem space and give the user a face and a voice. This user insight summarizes opinions given by multiple people during the user research phase.

Before and while they travel, travelers need access to information about local activities and authentic food available at their destination to have unique experiences while maximizing their time and budget.

Empathy Map

To see how the research findings translate into the way a person behaves, makes decisions, feels, and thinks, I put together an empathy map, which was a helpful step before defining the user persona.

Says:

“I travel to explore new places and have unique experiences.”

“When I travel, I like to go beyond the ultimate touristy spots.”

“When I visit a new destination, I want to go where the locals go walking or using public transportation.”

Does:

  • Spends several hours researching what non-touristy activities to do
  • Trust friends, acquaintances, and recommendations from locals about where to stay, what to do, and where to eat; uses reviews as selection tie-breakers
  • Plans trips far in advance to stay within budget
  • Prints itinerary information and saves it in phone notes for offline access

Thinks:

  • Where can I can information about what activities to do and where to eat?
  • Where can I find information regarding public transportation at my destination?
  • How do I make sure that I stay in safe neighborhoods?

Feels:

  • Happy researching his destination and talking to locals or people who have been there before 
  • Frustrated and deflated when the information he is looking for is not available online or is only available in other languages
  • Anxious about not knowing what clothes to bring given the weather and activities that he plans to do when traveling

Defining a user persona based on research

With a better understanding of the behavioral and cognitive processes of potential users, I proceeded to create a persona, which was the guiding force of ideation and prototyping.

To further determine the scope of the problem, I created a user scenario that added specific constraints to the persona’s situation and goals. This scenario was part of a larger client journey map that identified feelings, pain points, opportunities. The full client journey map can download here.

Scenario

Marco is 34 years old. He is a Civil Engineer and lives in Portland, Oregon. Marco just booked his flight to Lima, Peru. Now, he is planning activities for his trip. Unfortunately, he is having a hard time finding authentic and unique experiences.

He keeps finding the same type of activities in the different travel sites and nearly zero information about locals’ favorite restaurants and breweries.

Marco is feeling deflated and fears ending up visiting all the places that are meant for tourists and miss the uniqueness of Lima.

Goals

  • Having authentic and unique experiences while traveling
  • Maximizing his time and budget choosing the right things to do and places to eat
  • Staying away from tourist traps and get to discover the essence of a place through authentic food and beer

Gathering information from competitors to find out if others already address the user needs

Competitive Analysis: 

To ideate solutions that would help the user achieve his goals, I performed a competitive analysis to figure out if other products were addressing the user needs and, if so, how they were doing it.

I was able to find a direct and indirect competitor and performed a strengths and weaknesses assessment of their products as they relate to the user’s goals.

Although both competitors have strengths, they are not truly addressing the user pain points.

Direct competitor:
Jetzy - Travel like a local

Strengths
  • Tagline communicates the value proposition
  • Allows direct communication between users
Weaknesses
  • Unnecessary entry-point friction
  • Inconsistent UI
  • The user flow does not lead to finding information to travel like a local
  • Does not allow to save relevant information shared by other users

Indirect Competitor:
Forums, Tripadvisor - Read, review, compare prices and book

Strengths
  • A simple and intuitive interface, conducive to finding authentic information
  • Secures authentic information by other users
  • Reduces friction at the entry point
Weaknesses
  • No way to determine locals’ info vs. travelers’ info and no way to filter by locals’ recommendations
  • No way to download information saved for offline use

How might we address the user's pain point and help him achieve his goals?

With a good understanding of the persona, his needs and goals, and the fact that the competitors are not addressing the user needs, I proceeded to define a “How Might We” statement to guide the ideation process.

How might we help users find authentic information about unique local experiences available at their destinations?

Ideating solutions to solve the user pain points

Ideation and Prioritization

With the persona created in mind, the ideation process begins.

I used the "I like, I wish, What if" method to generate ideas aimed to solve the pain points of this user persona. His goals, habits, must-haves, frustrations, and personality are at the forefront of ideation.

As a next step, I used a prioritization matrix to identify what ideas could potentially become features based on users' satisfaction and their viability/feasibility for PukkaTrip as a company.

I like...

  • Having access to my travel info on my phone and offline

I wish...

  • I could connect with locals when I’m planning my trip
  • I could always get information that is up to date
  • I could save and revisit information about local activities that I want to do at my destination

What if...

  • I could poll locals about their favorite activities, restaurants, and breweries.
  • Responding to questions other travelers have about the area where I live, would give me some sort of benefit

Prioritization of the features that came out of the ideation phase took a couple of iterations. I focused on features that were key as they directly allow the user to accomplish his goal. By limiting the number of features, I came up with a minimum valuable product.

Revised feature prioritization

A challenge

At this point in the ideation process, I realized that integrating a poll feature that allows users to post questions and receive individualized responses would address the user’s pain points and make it stand out from competitors.

However, a poll feature requires users to engage with the app by responding to other travelers’ polls, which makes the functionality sustainable and keeps the information authentic. Answering to other users' questions is a big ask that could deter some potential users from using the app.

To move forward, I took a close look at the user persona and concluded that a poll feature would be well adopted by my user based on the following assumptions.

Assumptions

  • The user is a traveler who plans ahead and enjoys discovering and connecting with others
  • The user is willing to take extra steps to make sure he gets the information he needs to maximize his travel experience

Important considerations for the adoption of the poll feature

  • The user will be shown previous poll responses and other users’ contributions before committing to submit a poll
  • After sending a poll the user will be invited to, not required to, reply to a fellow traveler's poll
  • Replying to a poll has to be as effortless as possible. Language in the request should be carefully crafted to incentivize its use and boost user’s buy-in

User flow diagram

Once I decided to commit to including a poll feature, I began defining the user flow.

Goals

  • Defining a critical path that allowed the user to browse through locals’ and travelers’ recommendations
  • Allowing the user to submit a poll to get current and individualized answers to his questions and review poll responses
  • Figuring out a way to keep the community sustainable by encouraging users to provide authentic information to other users
  • Reducing friction at entry-point

    Note: This user flow incorporates the task of creating an account to submit a poll. Although it can be off-putting for some users, we are assuming that our user does most of his planning on his own and way in advance. Therefore, he is used to creating accounts to manage and keep track of the information he needs for his trip. There will be wording clarifying the convenience of creating an account on the screen.

Creating a user interface

User interface goals and considerations

  • Have a clear and unequivocal path to getting relevant information about experiences at a potential destination
  • An early introduction to facets: poll responses from travelers and locals, tips from locals, and categories of information available within those
  • Consistent and predictable UI patterns

Sketches

The first screen conveys the purpose of the app and allows the user to search for the information he needs right away by entering his destination.

Categories of information are introduced early and reinforced with descriptive words and icons.

Creating an account is only needed after valuable information has been shown to the user when he decides to submit a poll and to facilitate response tracking. The app respects user privacy and gives an alternative to location tracking.

The app allows creating a trip and associate tips and recommendations to it to increase convenience for users who are planners and appreciate organization.

The app encourages users to support other travelers by replying to their polls without forcing them to do so.

The account/profile screen has a simple interface that shows the user how to interact with the app. Here users will be able to see created trips and polls as well as responses sent and received.

The first round of usability testing

Before moving on to hi-fi prototyping, I sought feedback on these initial sketches to test the user flow. I got fascinating feedback.

Usability testing takeaways

  • Users were excited about the poll feature but thought it was hard to discover
  • Users felt overwhelmed by the number of filters shown and thought they took too much space on the screen
  • Users found that signing up with existent accounts like Google or Facebook was convenient
  • Users like the prompt to create a trip to organize all information related to their destination

Iterating based on users’ feedback

Wireframes

Incorporating the feedback received in round one of usability testing, I worked on Figma to design hi-fi wireframes and a prototype.

The first screen encountered by the user has a simple and unequivocal call to action that reveals the value proposition of PukkaTrip: search a destination and find authentic local experiences. All while incorporating an auto-complete search functionality.

Search results allow users to discover information facets by displaying icons with labels. Users can customize results by tapping "More filters" and selecting relevant filters. A pop-up message appears at the bottom of the screen to invite users to submit a poll question for personalized information.

Users are encouraged to reply to another user's poll after sending theirs. Responding is made simple and can be completed on the same screen. Users have the option to change the poll question or to complete this step later.

Users can see their account, which shows gives them access to trips created, searching for new destinations, and sent poll questions and responses. A notification icon shows poll responses received. Users see responses received to each poll question and can save relevant ones to the same or a new trip for easy access.

The second round of usability testing: Making sure that the prototype works as intended

To test the user flow, I performed usability testing with four users keeping in mind the defined user scenario.

The main goal for the usability test was to evaluate if the app allowed users to find information about an authentic local experience at his/her vacation travel destination.

User tasks

  • Describe what the app is for
  • Search a destination and apply filters
  • Send a poll
  • Respond to a fellow traveler’s poll 
  • Review received poll responses
  • Add poll responses to a trip

Usability testing takeaways

  • Users were able to describe the value proposition of the app by looking at the first couple of screens
  • Users were willing to contribute to the community by submitting a response to a poll and thought it was a simple step
  • Users liked the ability to add information found on the app to a specific trip
  • The search could be more sophisticated to accommodate advanced users
  • The visual design needs to be improved to facilitate the usability of filters on the search results page and access to other functionalities such as reviewing poll responses
  • Refining language around benefits for creating an account before submitting a poll could help justify burden and incentivize account creation

Translating user feedback into design improvements and producing a hi-fi prototype

Ideation iteration

After receiving valuable feedback from the user and analyzing the results, I translated the takeaways into design goals and revised the wireframes

Iteration goals

  • Preserve critical path since it was successful in helping users accomplish their goal
  • Improve visual design to address usability and discoverability issues by getting rid of small font sizes and increasing button contrast
  • Add features aimed to increase efficiency for advance users
  • Revise language around the need to create an account to use critical app functionality

Potential success metrics for a product like PukkaTrip

Even though PukkaTrip was a conceptual project, potential KPIs for this app could be:

  • A decrease in user’s time spent finding information about activities that locals do at their destinations
  • Increased user’s confidence in their ability to find an authentic and unique experience when using the app
  • Increased user satisfaction with experiences enjoyed during their trip
  • Growth in size of user/traveler community connected by the app
  • User’s sustained engagement and contribution to the app via poll responses and recommendations

Potential next steps for PukkaTrip

Potential next steps for PukkaTrip could be:

  • Test critical path with a larger number of users with similar goals of those of the user persona
  • Give users the option to apply filters before they search so they are directed to more relevant information as soon as possible
  • Design alternative user paths that could result in increased conversion; for example, by designing a path to save relevant information to a trip without submitting a poll
