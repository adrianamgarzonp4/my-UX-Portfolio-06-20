Project overview

Context

The National Park Service (NPS) preserves natural and cultural resources of 419 areas (including national parks, monuments, rivers, battlefields, military parks,etc.) covering 85 million acres across all states and some territories.

Areas have websites that live within the NPS site; hence, this site has two levels of navigation. The global navigation used for global pages (non-specific to an area) and the parks' navigation, which is the name that we are giving to the navigation found on area-specific pages or park pages.

Summary

Through heuristics evaluation and usability testing, our team concluded that the NPS website can be confusing to navigate due to its complex and overcategorized information architecture, which is inconsistent on both mobile and desktop as well as between park pages, putting a heavy burden on the user to learn a new information system every time they consult a new park page.

Also, the site's user interface design forces the user to recall where relevant information is located and has an unclear hierarchy and cluttered content design. Lastly, the site fails to provide flexibility and efficiency of use when it comes to finding a park, maybe the most important user flow on the site.

Our team redesigned the mobile and desktop National Park Service experience to offer a more organized information architecture, featuring consistent navigation across all pages and facilitating finding a park based on user's interests, park, or state name.

Project Goals

  • Uncover information architecture and user interface design issues on the NPS site through usability testing and heuristic evaluation
  • Redesign the nps.gov user experience on desktop and mobile for a better user experience

Methodology

We used the UX design methodology with an emphasis on usability heuristics and testing. Once we identified the main issues, my teammate and I spent most of our time redesigning the information architecture and ideating UI patterns. Then we wireframed and prototyped our proposed solution.

Heuristics evaluation on critical user flow

Main heuristics issues on critical user flow

  • The navigation’s content can be extremely overwhelming and create overchoice.
  • Navigation labels don’t speak the user’s language, rather, they are focused on the agency language and the user is asked to select from a wide range of options that might not mean anything to him/her.
  • UI on global navigation is inconsistent.
  • Navigation options are inconsistent across park pages making the site unpredictable and unlearnable.
  • The site lacks user control, it relays only on the browser back button and does not provide the user a way to change or adjust his/her selection from search. This results in the user having to restart the search process to find another park in the same state.
  • The site's intricate IA, could make users feel anxious about navigating away from pages for fear to loosing their place. User might not know whether they will be able to get to the same place again.

Usability testing

Goal

Learning how the user interacts with nps.gov while trying to find information about specific parks in Arkansas.

User tasks

  • Find national parks in Arkansas
  • Find Buffalo National River
  • Find Buffalo operating hours
  • Find a different National park in Arkansas
  • Find operating hours in the second park page of the user's choice
  • Go back to Buffalo and find operating hours using navigation options not used before (icon navigation or dropdown navigation)

Usability notes and affinity grouping on Miro

  • "It took me a minute to realize that this was the National Parks Service website! I wish that 'Find a Park' were more prominent."
  • "I don't understand why the information about hours of operation is not consistent in all pages."
  • "Some of the pictures aren't as related to the text and are a bit distracting from the content and headers."
  • "I feel that there is soo much information on this site. The information isn't bad, it's just disorganized. It seems to me that even after browsing for a long time, I still can't find all the information I need to plan a visit."

Usability testing takeaways

  • Users were initially confused about the site’s purpose
  • Navigation is overly complex and inconsistent across park pages on the same state
  • The search leads to results that are not relevant
  • Users have to perform the same steps multiple time to select a different search result

Heuristics evaluation of navigation structure

Since one of the main findings from the usability testing had to do with a negative experience with the site’s navigation, we decided to take a closer look at navigation and information architecture.

Heuristics navigation issues

  • Lack of consistent navigation across park pages
  • Not easy to identify your location on the site and hard to recall paths
  • Unclear hierarchy leads the user to think hard about where to go to find the necessary information
  • Inconsistent UI in content pages leads to user confusion
  • Confusing combination of text navigation and icon-based navigation on desktop site

Making sense of heuristic evaluations and usability testing

My teammate and I regrouped to look at our findings from usability testing a heuristics evaluations to make sure we were on the same page about the problem that we were trying to solve.

Defining the scope of the problem based on research

The current national park service can be overall confusing to navigate because:

  • Both global and park navigation are cluttered and inconsistent in UI design and content
  • Global and park pages have an unclear hierarchy and cluttered content design
  • Searching for a park lacks user control and over relays on the back button of the browser instead of giving the user control over their search results

Defining the site redesign goals to address main IA and UI design issues

Our research gave us a clear understanding of the scope of the problem. With that in mind, we defined the redesign goals for our project using a prioritization matrix.

Prioratizarion matrix

Redesign Goals

  • Redesign the information architecture on global and park pages to clarify the site purpose and showcase relevant information for the user
  • Remove from navigation any items that without context make the navigation less usable and put the burden on the user to figure out meaning and importance concerning his/her goals
  • Design an autocomplete search for finding a park to help users more easily discover parks and activities
  • Improve the visual design of navigation, search, and UI elements, ensure typographic hierarchy, and make the site easier to scan to improve the site's usability

Redesigning the information architecture of the navigation

Card sorting exercise

We started by taking a look at the existing information architecture. We found overcategorization on the parks' pages as well as on overly complex navigation structure that makes it difficult for users to find what they need. Some categories do not seem to follow any principles of organization and seem to be under one category simply because they did not fit in another category.

Global existing navigation structure

Park Pages existing navigation structure overview

Park Pages existing navigation structure detail

To improve the sites information architecture we performed a card sorting exercise and we made the following decisions:

  • Revised information hierarchy
  • Eliminated options that delay decision-making, removing overchoice or categories that seem useless without context
  • Included options that would give park web content managers the flexibility to incorporate area-specific content without sacrificing the navigation consistency across park pages

KPIs

Potential success metrics for the redesigned NPS website

Even though this redesign was a conceptual project, potential KPIs for this website could be:

  • Increase in user’s awareness of NPS.gov purpose and value
  • Reduction in the amount of time that users find themselves reading navigation options and deciding what page to visit
  • Increase in user’s ability to recall paths used to access relevant information
  • Increase in user's sentiment of control over search results
  • Increase user’s ability to tell where they are in the site
